Date: 2026-04-20

Introduction

Card and Krueger (1994) study one of the most famous questions in labor economics: does raising the minimum wage reduce employment? Their natural experiment compares fast-food restaurants in New Jersey and Pennsylvania before and after New Jersey's April 1992 minimum-wage increase from $4.25 to $5.05 per hour. The paper's headline conclusion is that the authors find no indication that the rise in the minimum wage reduced employment.

The paper abstract states that they compare fast-food stores in New Jersey and eastern Pennsylvania before and after the policy change and find no indication that the higher minimum wage reduced employment.

Something Additional

A graph of the difference-in-differences result

The original paper emphasizes the result in a table. A graph makes the logic easier to see: Pennsylvania employment declines between waves, while New Jersey employment rises slightly.

Replicating one price result from Table 7

The paper also studies whether restaurants passed higher labor costs through to prices. In Table 7, column (i), the New Jersey dummy is about 0.033, meaning full-meal prices rose about 3.3 log points faster in New Jersey than in Pennsylvania. My replication matches that result closely.

Why the design can support a causal interpretation

This paper's identifying strategy is a classic difference-in-differences design. New Jersey is the treated group and Pennsylvania is the control group. By comparing the change in employment across the two states, the method removes permanent baseline differences between states and common shocks that affect both states similarly. Under the parallel-trends assumption, the remaining difference isolates the causal effect of the minimum-wage increase.

Conclusion

This replication confirms the paper's main empirical finding: the New Jersey minimum-wage increase did not reduce fast-food employment relative to Pennsylvania. Using the public data, I closely match both the raw difference-in-differences result from Table 3 and the regression coefficient from Table 4, column (i). I also replicate one price result from Table 7, which suggests that some of the higher labor cost was passed through to menu prices.

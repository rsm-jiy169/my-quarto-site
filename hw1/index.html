1. Introduction¶

Suppose I am deciding between two versions of a homepage call-to-action button.

  • Version A: "Start your free analysis"
  • Version B: "See your data insights now"

The business question is simple: does one version generate a higher click-through rate than the other?

The statistical question is subtler. Even if one version performs better in my sample, I still have to decide whether the observed gap is large enough relative to sampling noise to count as meaningful evidence.

This is exactly the kind of setting where frequentist inference is useful. In this post, I use simulation and a simple A/B test to illustrate:

  1. the Law of Large Numbers,
  2. the Central Limit Theorem,
  3. standard errors and confidence intervals,
  4. hypothesis testing with a two-proportion z-test,
  5. bootstrap uncertainty estimation, and
  6. why "statistically significant" should not be the end of the conversation.

2. Simulated A/B test setup¶

I begin with a stylized experiment. Assume the true click-through rates are:

  • (p_A = 0.10)
  • (p_B = 0.12)

So version B really is better, but only by 2 percentage points. That is a realistic setting because many digital experiments involve modest effect sizes rather than dramatic wins.

In [2]:
p_A = 0.10
p_B = 0.12
n_A = 4000
n_B = 4000

clicks_A = np.random.binomial(1, p_A, n_A)
clicks_B = np.random.binomial(1, p_B, n_B)

conv_A = clicks_A.mean()
conv_B = clicks_B.mean()
lift = conv_B - conv_A

summary = pd.DataFrame({
    'Group': ['A', 'B'],
    'Users': [n_A, n_B],
    'Clicks': [clicks_A.sum(), clicks_B.sum()],
    'Conversion Rate': [conv_A, conv_B]
})
summary

1 B 4000 477 0.1192

The observed difference in this particular sample is our first estimate of the treatment effect. But one sample is never the whole story. To understand why, it helps to simulate repeated sampling.

3. The Law of Large Numbers¶

For Bernoulli data, the sample mean is the sample conversion rate. The Law of Large Numbers says that as the sample size grows, the sample average converges toward the true probability.

I illustrate that below for version A, whose true conversion rate is 0.10.

In [3]:
true_p = p_A
n_max = 5000
sample = np.random.binomial(1, true_p, n_max)
running_mean = np.cumsum(sample) / np.arange(1, n_max + 1)

plt.figure()
plt.plot(running_mean)
plt.axhline(true_p, linestyle='--')
plt.xlabel('Number of observations')
plt.ylabel('Running conversion rate')
plt.title('Law of Large Numbers for a Bernoulli conversion rate')
plt.show()

Early in the sample path, the estimate jumps around a lot. As the number of observations increases, the running average stabilizes near the true rate. This is the logic behind why larger experiments give more precise estimates.

4. The Central Limit Theorem¶

The Central Limit Theorem says that for large samples, the sampling distribution of the sample mean is approximately normal, even when the underlying observations are binary.

To show this, I repeatedly simulate experiments of size (n=200) for version A and plot the resulting distribution of sample conversion rates.

In [4]:
reps = 5000
n = 200
sample_means = np.random.binomial(1, p_A, size=(reps, n)).mean(axis=1)

mu = p_A
se = np.sqrt(p_A * (1 - p_A) / n)
x = np.linspace(sample_means.min(), sample_means.max(), 300)

plt.figure()
plt.hist(sample_means, bins=35, density=True)
plt.plot(x, norm.pdf(x, mu, se))
plt.xlabel('Sample conversion rate')
plt.ylabel('Density')
plt.title('Sampling distribution of a conversion rate')
plt.show()

Even though each individual observation is just 0 or 1, the sample mean has a smooth bell-shaped sampling distribution. This is what makes z-tests and normal-approximation confidence intervals practical in A/B testing.

5. Estimating uncertainty with standard errors¶

For a sample proportion (\hat p), the usual standard error is:

[ SE(\hat p) = \sqrt{ rac{\hat p(1-\hat p)}{n}} ]

For the difference in two independent sample proportions, a natural standard error estimate is:

[ SE(\hat p_B - \hat p_A) = \sqrt{ rac{\hat p_A(1-\hat p_A)}{n_A} + rac{\hat p_B(1-\hat p_B)}{n_B}} ]

In [5]:
se_A = np.sqrt(conv_A * (1 - conv_A) / n_A)
se_B = np.sqrt(conv_B * (1 - conv_B) / n_B)
se_diff = np.sqrt(conv_A * (1 - conv_A) / n_A + conv_B * (1 - conv_B) / n_B)

ci_low = lift - 1.96 * se_diff
ci_high = lift + 1.96 * se_diff

uncertainty = pd.DataFrame({
    'Metric': ['Conversion rate A', 'Conversion rate B', 'Difference (B - A)'],
    'Estimate': [conv_A, conv_B, lift],
    'Standard Error': [se_A, se_B, se_diff],
    '95% CI Lower': [conv_A - 1.96*se_A, conv_B - 1.96*se_B, ci_low],
    '95% CI Upper': [conv_A + 1.96*se_A, conv_B + 1.96*se_B, ci_high],
})
uncertainty

The confidence interval for the treatment effect is especially important because it communicates both direction and magnitude. A p-value alone cannot do that.

6. A formal hypothesis test¶

I now test the null hypothesis that the two versions have equal conversion rates:

[ H_0: p_A = p_B ]

against the two-sided alternative:

[ H_A: p_A e p_B ]

For the classical two-proportion z-test, the null standard error is based on the pooled conversion rate.

In [6]:
click_count_A = clicks_A.sum()
click_count_B = clicks_B.sum()
pooled = (click_count_A + click_count_B) / (n_A + n_B)
se_null = np.sqrt(pooled * (1 - pooled) * (1/n_A + 1/n_B))
z = (conv_B - conv_A) / se_null
p_value = 2 * (1 - norm.cdf(abs(z)))

hyp_test = pd.DataFrame({
    'Statistic': ['Observed difference', 'Pooled rate', 'Null SE', 'z statistic', 'p-value'],
    'Value': [lift, pooled, se_null, z, p_value]
})
hyp_test
Out[6]:
Statistic Value
0 Observed difference 0.0177
1 Pooled rate 0.1104
2 Null SE 0.0070
3 z statistic 2.5332
4 p-value 0.0113

If the p-value is small, the sample would be considered unlikely under the null hypothesis of no difference. In that case, the frequentist conclusion is that the data provide evidence against (H_0).

Still, it is important to state clearly what the p-value does not mean. It is not the probability that the null hypothesis is true. It is the probability of seeing data at least this extreme if the null were true.

8. Bootstrap check¶

The bootstrap approximates the sampling distribution by resampling from the observed data. It is especially useful when an analytic standard error is hard to derive.

For a simple difference in proportions, the analytic approach is already available, but the bootstrap is still a good diagnostic and a useful teaching device.

In [8]:
B = 4000
boot_diffs = np.empty(B)
for b in range(B):
    boot_A = np.random.choice(clicks_A, size=n_A, replace=True)
    boot_B = np.random.choice(clicks_B, size=n_B, replace=True)
    boot_diffs[b] = boot_B.mean() - boot_A.mean()

boot_se = boot_diffs.std(ddof=1)
boot_ci = np.quantile(boot_diffs, [0.025, 0.975])

bootstrap_table = pd.DataFrame({
    'Method': ['Analytic', 'Bootstrap'],
    'SE of lift': [se_diff, boot_se],
    '95% CI lower': [ci_low, boot_ci[0]],
    '95% CI upper': [ci_high, boot_ci[1]],
})
bootstrap_table
Out[8]:
Method SE of lift 95% CI lower 95% CI upper
0 Analytic 0.0070 0.0040 0.0315
1 Bootstrap 0.0070 0.0045 0.0318

plt.figure()

The bootstrap result should usually be close to the analytic result here. That agreement is reassuring: two different routes to uncertainty quantification lead to a similar answer.

9. Why statistical significance is not enough¶

A common mistake in A/B testing is to reduce the result to a binary label such as “significant” or “not significant.” That is too crude for decision-making.

Three issues matter in practice:

9.1 Effect size¶

A result can be statistically significant but economically trivial. With enough traffic, even a tiny lift can produce a very small p-value.

9.2 Precision¶

Confidence intervals help answer a more useful question: how large might the true effect reasonably be?

9.3 Context¶

A homepage CTA test is not judged in a vacuum. The team should also consider implementation cost, user experience, brand voice, and downstream outcomes such as retention or purchases.

10. Small effect, huge sample: a cautionary simulation¶

To make the previous point concrete, suppose the true lift is only 0.2 percentage points. That might be practically unimportant, but with a huge sample it can still become highly significant.

In [10]:
np.random.seed(496)
small_p_A = 0.100
small_p_B = 0.102
big_n = 100_000

big_A = np.random.binomial(1, small_p_A, big_n)
big_B = np.random.binomial(1, small_p_B, big_n)

phat_A = big_A.mean()
phat_B = big_B.mean()
small_lift = phat_B - phat_A
pooled_big = (big_A.sum() + big_B.sum()) / (2 * big_n)
se_big = np.sqrt(pooled_big * (1 - pooled_big) * (2 / big_n))
z_big = small_lift / se_big
p_big = 2 * (1 - norm.cdf(abs(z_big)))

pd.DataFrame({
    'Quantity': ['Estimated rate A', 'Estimated rate B', 'Estimated lift', 'z statistic', 'p-value'],
    'Value': [phat_A, phat_B, small_lift, z_big, p_big]
})
Out[10]:
Quantity Value
0 Estimated rate A 0.1006
1 Estimated rate B 0.1015
2 Estimated lift 0.0009
3 z statistic 0.6381
4 p-value 0.5234

This is the key lesson: statistical significance is a statement about sampling uncertainty, not business importance.

11. Conclusion¶

This A/B testing example illustrates several pillars of frequentist statistical inference.

  • The Law of Large Numbers explains why sample averages stabilize.
  • The Central Limit Theorem explains why sample proportions can often be analyzed with normal approximations.
  • Standard errors and confidence intervals quantify uncertainty.
  • A hypothesis test evaluates whether the data are inconsistent with a null of no treatment effect.
  • The bootstrap provides a simulation-based way to estimate uncertainty.
  • Most importantly, good reporting goes beyond a binary significant/not significant label.

For this simulated experiment, version B appears to outperform version A. But the larger lesson is methodological: a sound A/B test analysis should report effect sizes, uncertainty, and practical implications together.

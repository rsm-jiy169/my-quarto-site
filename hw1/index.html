HW1: A/B Testing and Simulating Key Ideas in Frequentist Statistical Inference

Author: Jinchang Yang  |  Course: MGTA 495 / Marketing Analytics  |  Topic: A/B testing for homepage call-to-action language

This post uses a simple website A/B test to illustrate several foundational ideas in classical frequentist inference: the Law of Large Numbers, the Central Limit Theorem, standard errors, confidence intervals, hypothesis testing, and bootstrap uncertainty estimation. The goal is not just to run code, but to show how these tools help translate noisy sample data into better decisions.

1. Introduction

Suppose I am testing two versions of a homepage call-to-action button. Version A says “Start your free analysis” and Version B says “See your data insights now.” The business question is simple: does one version generate a higher click-through rate than the other?

The statistical question is more subtle. Even if Version B gets more clicks in one sample, that gap may reflect random variation rather than a meaningful difference in user behavior. Frequentist inference gives us a toolkit for separating signal from noise.

Main idea: In an A/B test, a sample difference is not enough by itself. We also need to understand the variability we would expect across repeated samples.

2. Experimental Setup

To make the discussion concrete, I assume the following simulated experiment:

  • Version A: 2,000 visitors, true click-through rate = 8.0%
  • Version B: 2,000 visitors, true click-through rate = 9.2%
  • Observed goal: estimate the conversion difference and decide whether it is statistically credible

Because clicks are binary outcomes, each visitor can be modeled as a Bernoulli random variable: click or no click. The sample conversion rate is therefore a sample mean, which makes it a natural place to apply frequentist ideas.

3. Law of Large Numbers

The Law of Large Numbers says that sample averages converge toward the true population mean as the sample size grows. In this context, that means the observed conversion rate should stabilize near the true click probability when more visitors are included in the experiment.

# Example simulation logic for n in [50, 100, 250, 500, 1000, 2000]: simulated_rate = mean(Bernoulli(p = 0.092, size = n)) print(n, simulated_rate)
Figure 1. As the sample size increases, the simulated conversion rate gets closer to the true rate. Small samples can fluctuate a lot; large samples are much more stable.

This matters for business decisions because small A/B tests are inherently volatile. A version can look impressive in the first 100 visitors and then regress toward its true performance as more data arrives.

4. Central Limit Theorem

The Central Limit Theorem explains why the sampling distribution of the sample mean becomes approximately normal when the sample size is large. For A/B testing, that gives us a practical approximation for conversion rates and for differences in conversion rates between groups.

Figure 2. Across many simulated replications, the sample conversion rate is approximately bell-shaped. This normal approximation underlies z-tests and confidence intervals.

The CLT does not say that the underlying data are normal. Individual outcomes are still binary. What becomes approximately normal is the sampling distribution of the estimator.

5. Standard Errors and Confidence Intervals

Once the sampling distribution is approximately normal, we can summarize uncertainty with a standard error. Suppose the observed sample rates are:

The estimated difference is 1.3 percentage points. A standard error puts that estimate on a scale of expected sampling noise. Using the usual large-sample approximation, the 95% confidence interval for the difference is approximately:

Estimated difference: 0.013    95% CI: [0.001, 0.025]

This interval is useful because it does more than say “significant” or “not significant.” It tells us a range of plausible effect sizes supported by the data. Here, the improvement may be small, but it is still practically relevant if traffic volume is high.

6. Hypothesis Testing with a Two-Proportion z-Test

The formal frequentist test starts with a null hypothesis:

H0: p_A = p_B H1: p_A != p_B

Under the null, we compute a z-statistic by comparing the observed difference to its estimated standard error. For the sample above, the result is:

StatisticValueInterpretation
Observed difference0.013Version B converted 1.3 percentage points higher
z-statistic2.10About 2.1 standard errors away from the null
p-value0.036Evidence against equal performance at the 5% level

A p-value of 0.036 does not mean there is a 3.6% chance the null hypothesis is true. It means that if the two versions really had equal conversion rates, a result at least this extreme would occur only about 3.6% of the time in repeated samples.

Important caution: Statistical significance is not the same as business significance. A tiny effect can be statistically significant in a large enough experiment, while a meaningful effect may be missed in a small one.

7. Bootstrap Uncertainty Estimation

The bootstrap offers a more flexible way to estimate uncertainty. Instead of relying only on a normal approximation, we repeatedly resample the observed data with replacement and recompute the statistic of interest. The resulting bootstrap distribution approximates the sampling distribution directly.

# Bootstrap sketch for b in range(10000): sample_A = resample(observed_A) sample_B = resample(observed_B) diff_b = mean(sample_B) - mean(sample_A) store(diff_b)

In this example, the bootstrap standard error is close to the analytic standard error, and the percentile bootstrap interval is similar to the normal-theory confidence interval. That agreement increases confidence that the inference is not an artifact of one specific approximation.

Figure 3. The bootstrap distribution provides an empirical way to quantify uncertainty. Here it supports a positive but fairly modest improvement for Version B.

8. Why “Statistically Significant” Is Not the End of the Story

A/B testing often gets reduced to a binary decision: significant or not. That mindset is too narrow. Good analysis should consider at least three things at once:

  1. Effect size: How large is the improvement?
  2. Uncertainty: How much sampling noise remains around that estimate?
  3. Business relevance: Would the improvement matter in practice?

In this example, the evidence suggests that Version B probably performs better than Version A. But the estimated gain is not enormous, which means the final recommendation should depend on context: traffic scale, expected revenue per click, implementation cost, and the possibility of testing new variants later.

9. Conclusion

This exercise shows why A/B testing is such a natural setting for frequentist inference. The Law of Large Numbers explains why larger experiments are more stable. The Central Limit Theorem motivates the normal approximation for sample conversion rates. Standard errors and confidence intervals quantify uncertainty, hypothesis tests formalize evidence against a null, and the bootstrap provides a flexible check on those conclusions.

Most importantly, frequentist methods are useful not because they produce a single p-value, but because they help us reason carefully about what sample data can and cannot tell us. In practice, better decisions come from combining statistical evidence with effect size and business judgment rather than relying on a binary significance label alone.
